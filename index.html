WHAT IS GESTALT THERAPY?

Gestalt therapy is a practical and highly effective therapy aimed at integrating an individual's mind and body. By working through the client's emotions and physical sensations in the “Here and Now,” it helps to bring to light new perspectives (awareness) on a new way of life and creates transformations in behavior. It helps individuals break free from blindly accepting things as they appear (introjection) or suppressing their emotions, achieving mind-body integration and living autonomously as one's true existential self. By living in this natural state, relationships with others also change, which creates harmony. Gestalt therapy can bring significant benefits not only to individuals but also to groups and organizations. In group work, I have witnessed individuals who start as strangers gradually grow into a supportive and warm group, helping each other along the way.

THE BEGINNING OF GESTALT THERAPY

Gestalt therapy was developed in the 1950s by Frederick “Fritz” (1893-1979) and Laura (1905-1990) Perls along with Paul Goodman (1911-1972). Frits Perls wrote “Gestalt Therapy” in 1951 and established the Gestalt Institute in New York in the early 1950s. It became popular and expanded significantly in the 1960s and 1970s in the United States.

Fritz had studied under Sigmund Freud in Germany, but later parted ways with Freudian psychotherapy and adopted a more existential and humanistic psychological approach, focusing on the individual and their awareness.

Today, Gestalt therapy is recognized by the International Association for Advanced Gestalt Therapy (IAAGT), and practiced internationally, with research institutes in many places, including in New York, Cleveland, Pittsburg, Toronto, Ireland, and London. It is taught in universities as an academic subject and is highly regarded overseas for its practical, positive, and effective approach to behavioral change.

GESTALT THERAPY PRACTICE

The flow of a session, whether individual or in a group setting, starts with a “check-in,” where the client talks about their feelings and physical sensations in the “Here and Now” and their expectations of the day. In Gestalt therapy, individual counseling is called “work.” In the work itself, a client can express the most pressing issue what s/he is experiencing in the “Here and Now.” This takes about an hour. At the end of the session, there is a “check-out,” where the client reflects on their feelings after the day. If there are any lingering emotions, it is important to leave them on the therapy site.

There are four ways to start “work” in Gestalt therapy: ① specific issue that are of concern, ② physical discomfort, ③ dreams, and ④ art (such as drawings, collage or cards). The most important aspect of “work” is for the client to focus on what they are feeling or thinking in the “Here and Now”.

“Work” may involve quietly sitting still through a conversation, while other time it may involve various activities through experimental suggestions by the therapist. One traditional technique used is called the “Empty Chair” method. The therapist will never lead the client in any way. Instead, the therapist will be with the client to draw their attention to their feelings in the “Here and Now” and encourage self-awareness. Fritz said that through self-integration, clients come to see themselves as part of the world, and through recognizing their connection with others, they are able to make good contact with their surroudings. Fritz wished for people to have Phenomenological point of view, in other words, to not see others through the lens of personal values, biases or societal norms, but to see them in their raw, authentic existence. Fritz believed that by adopting such a phenomenological perspective, one could avoid the hindrances created by one's own assumptions.

ゲシュタルト・セラピーの実践

セッションの流れは、個人であれ、グループ・ワークショップであれ、まず、チェック・インをしていただきます。チェック・インとは「今、 ここ」の気持ちや身体の感覚を話し、またワークで何をしたいか。話したい特定の問題があるか、身体に気になるところがあるか等、自由にお話しいただきます。 そして「ワーク」と呼ばれるゲシュタルト・セラピーのカウンセリング自体においては「今、ここ」で一番気になることをお話ししていただきます。 セッションの最後には、チェック・アウトをしていただきます。チェック・アウトでは、セッションが終わった「今、ここ」の気持ちを話します。 モヤモヤした気持ち等があれば、全てそれをこの場において帰ることが大切です

さて、肝心の「ワーク」には、4つの入り口があります。① 気になる具体的な問題　② 身体の不調　③ 夢 ④ アート（絵、コラージュ、カード）等からワークに入 ることが出来ます。一番大切なことは、あなたが「今、ここ」で感じている事、気になっている事、心に溜まっていること等をテーマにすることです。静かに座ったままのワ ークもあれば、セラピストの実験的提案によって多様に動くこともあります。エンプティ・チェアという技法を使うこともあります。セラピストが勝手にクライアント を誘導することはありません。セラピストは、ゆっくりとクライアントの「今、ここ」の気持ちに寄り添って、クライアント自身が自己の気持ちや感情を認識するすることによる 「気づき」を促してゆきます。これは、クライアント自身が自分自身に関わるという関係性を促すプロセスです

それは、ソクラテスの「産婆術」に似ているかもしれません。セラピストは、クライアント自身が自分自身に関わるという関係性のプロセスに関わるのです。 そのプロセスからクライアントの自己統合（例えば、葛藤していた自分が統合される）の「瞬間」が生まれます。クライアントの実存としての統合の瞬間、時満ちた瞬間を得て、 クライアントは現実的行動変容を得るのです。この瞬間をゲシュタルト療法推進国際協会（IAAGT）のNewsletter投稿者達は、正に“Gestalt’s magic”いう言葉で表現しております。 これが弊会の名前G’s Magic Tokyoの由来です。クライアントのこの「自分が自分と関係することによって、統合へと向かう関係性」、セラピストは、その関係性自体に真摯に関わるのです。 「クライアントが自己統合することによって、自身を世界の中の一部分であるとみることができるようになり、周囲と自分が繋がっていることの認識によって、良いコンタクトができるようになる」 とパールズは話しております。そして、現象学的視点を持つこと、つまり、自分の価値観、偏見、常識の色眼鏡で他者を観ず、他者のありのままの存在を観られるようになることを、パールズは願っていました。 なぜならば、そうした現象学的視点をもつことによって、自分自身の思い込みによる支障は回避されるからです。

KAYANO MIZUNO, PH.D.

Born in Tokyo in 1956, Dr. Mizuno studied at the Sacred Heart School from kindergarten through high school and graduated from the University of the Sacred Heart in Tokyo. She earned both a Master's degree in 1983 and a Ph.D. in Philosophy in 1992 from Boston College Graduate School. After returning to Japan, she worked for over 20 years at the U.S. financial firms. She has been studying Gestalt therapy since 2000, and has a lecture since 2017. Dr. Mizuno is an Industrial Counselor, a former Board member of the Japanese Association of Gestalt Therapy, a former Board member of the Japanese Association of Boston, and the former President of the Japan-America Women's Club. She currently leads Gestalt's Magic Tokyo (G's Magic).
